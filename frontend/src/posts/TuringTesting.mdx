This was initially part of the CAPTCHA project but it ended up being rather interesting on its own,
so here it is as a standalone article, detailing my quest to discern AI and bots from actual humans.

After the fact I stumbled upon [FLAIR](github.com/hongwang600/FLAIR), and its encouraging to see that
I found some of the same weaknesses that they have. 

## Word Association Pairs
The simplest method I can think of for verifying humanity is word associations.  
LLMs are normally good at this too but I think I can add a twist that throws them off by intentionally misspelling words.

### Example

Stitch, Surgeon  
Staple, Paperwork  
Speaker, Music  
Space, SciFi
Glass, Alcohol


Then from the 'correct' answer, we just send the words with random ordering and no pairing, and have the testee put them
in pairs.
For N words we get (N/2)!/2 combinations - pretty good. 

## Word Association Graphs
A word association graph could be much better - N^N, but its a much easier puzzle to fail, and more frustrating.  

### Example   
**Jesus, Dice, Wine, High-Fantasy, Vegas, Rome**  
Jesus: Wine, Rome  
Dice: High-Fantasy, Vegas    
Wine: Jesus, Rome   
High-Fantasy: Dice    
Vegas: Dice, Rome  
Rome: Vegas, Wine, Jesus  

It could potentially be made easier by allowing some number of 'mistakes', or not penalizing some number of extraneous 
associations.

### Example

# Bonus Points 

What if I wanted to actually make something like this viable at scale? Against a dedicated attacker? 

An algorithm that knows all the operations could be made to test all of them and find out which fit efficiently.   
I need some operations to require tasks that LLMs are normally good at but normal programs are bad at.  
I think the goal should be asymmetry - making the problem exponentially harder for the attacker than the verifier.  
It should also be fair to assume I'm having an LLM verify responses or at least pregenerate a list of acceptable ones.

This is fun so I came up with more challenges that are harder for brute force algorithms than LLMs.
The starred ones are also hard for LLMs but easy for them to verify. 

### Easy Things to Pregenerate And Mix In (Hard For Algorithmic Sovers)
- Another thing that is the same color as the first.
- Linear Numeric Reasoning  (what number or date satisfies x y and z conditions?) where the conditions are redundant or simple.
    For example , "What is the smallest number divisible by 7 and 8 that ends in 2 and is greater than 560,111."
- Basic mathematical reasoning (which number is bigger, etc.)

### Asymmetric Prompts (Hard for LLMS and Algorithmic Solvers, But Easy To Verify)
- Another word that when misspelled, that contains the letters of \{input\} but in a different order.    
- A thing that is also 2 types of things that \{input\} is. Answer like: type, type, thing    
- A word or phrase that poorly describes \{input\}, but is still applicable.  
- Badly Describe \{input\}.

## Simple Math 
### Prompt 
What is the volume of a cube with side lengths 2 minus the volume of a sphere with volume 1?

### Response 
<blockquote>
To solve this problem, we need to follow these steps:

Calculate the volume of the cube.
Use the given volume of the sphere to find its radius.
Calculate the volume of the sphere using its radius.
Subtract the volume of the sphere from the volume of the cube.
Let's break it down step by step.

The volume ( V_\{\text\{cube\}\} ) of a cube with side length 2 is given by:
[ V_\{\text\{cube\}\} = 2^3 = 8 ]

The volume ( V_\{\text\{sphere\}\} ) of a sphere is given by:
[ V_\{\text\{sphere\}\} = \frac\{4\}\{3\} \pi r^3 ]
We know that the volume of the sphere is 1, so we can solve for the radius ( r ):
[ 1 = \frac\{4\}\{3\} \pi r^3 ]
[ r^3 = \frac\{3\}\{4\pi\} ]
[ r = \left( \frac\{3\}\{4\pi\} \right)^\{1/3\} ]

We don't actually need the radius to find the volume of the sphere, as it is already given.

Finally, we subtract the volume of the sphere from the volume of the cube:
[ V_\{\text\{result\}\} = V_\{\text\{cube\}\} - V_\{\text\{sphere\}\} = 8 - 1 = 7 ]

Let's implement this in Python using sympy to ensure accuracy.

### Prompt 
The density of ice is 4% less than the density of water. If a 5 cubic meter sphere of ice is dropped into 10 cubic meters of water, how much liquid water is there?

### Response 
<blockquote>
    To determine how much liquid water there will be after dropping a 5 cubic meter sphere of ice into 10 cubic meters of water, we need to consider the displacement caused by the ice. Here are the steps:

    Understand the Density Relationship:

    The density of water is ( \rho_\{\text\{water\}\} ).
    The density of ice is ( \rho_\{\text\{ice\}\} = 0.96 \rho_\{\text\{water\}\} ) (since ice is 4% less dense than water).
    Volume Calculation:

    Volume of the ice sphere: ( V_\{\text\{ice\}\} = 5 ) cubic meters.
    Weight and Buoyancy:

    The weight of the ice is equal to its mass, which can be calculated as:
    [
    m_\{\text\{ice\}\} = \rho_\{\text\{ice\}\} \times V_\{\text\{ice\}\}
    ]
    The buoyant force (which equals the weight of the displaced water) must also be considered. For an object to float, the buoyant force must equal its weight.
    Volume Displaced:

    Let ( V_\{\text\{displaced\}\} ) be the volume of water displaced by the ice.
    Since the ice floats, it will displace a volume of water equal to its own mass divided by the density of water:
    [
    V_\{\text\{displaced\}\} = \frac\{m_\{\text\{ice\}\}\}\{\rho_\{\text\{water\}\}\} = \frac\{\rho_\{\text\{ice\}\} \times V_\{\text\{ice\}\}\}\{\rho_\{\text\{water\}\}\} = 0.96 \times 5 = 4.8 \text\{ cubic meters\}
    ]
    Final Water Volume Calculation:

    Initial volume of water: ( V_\{\text\{initial\}\} = 10 ) cubic meters.
    Volume of water displaced by the ice: ( V_\{\text\{displaced\}\} = 4.8 ) cubic meters.
    Therefore, the final volume of liquid water will be:
    [
    V_\{\text\{final\}\} = V_\{\text\{initial\}\} + V_\{\text\{ice\}\} - V_\{\text\{displaced\}\} = 10 + 5 - 4.8 = 10.2 \text\{ cubic meters\}
    ]
    Thus, after dropping the 5 cubic meter sphere of ice into 10 cubic meters of water, there will be 10.2 cubic meters of liquid water.
</blockquote>

# Conclusions

It's definitely possible to run asymmetric humanity challenges using an LLM verifier, I've demonstrated that some work here.  
I don't intend to personally for this website, it's overkill, but it could be a fun future project, and 
I believe this approach could certainly scale, though it should be guarded by an algorithmic challenge first to prevent DOS attacks.
